QUANTUM FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions about quantum computing and our services.

Quantum computing leverages quantum mechanical phenomena like superposition and entanglement to perform calculations. Unlike classical bits that are either 0 or 1, quantum bits (qubits) can exist in multiple states simultaneously, enabling quantum computers to solve certain complex problems exponentially faster than classical computers.

Quantum computing excels at problems involving optimization, simulation, and machine learning. Key applications include:

  • Molecular modeling for drug discovery
  • Financial portfolio optimization
  • Logistics and supply chain optimization
  • Cryptography and cybersecurity
  • Machine learning pattern recognition

Quantum computing is in the noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) era, with devices containing 50-100 qubits available through cloud platforms. While error correction remains a challenge, these devices can already solve practical problems, especially when combined with classical computing in hybrid approaches. Major tech companies and startups are rapidly advancing the technology, with quantum advantage (outperforming classical computers on practical problems) being demonstrated for specific applications.

Businesses can take several steps to prepare for quantum computing:

  • Identify potential use cases where quantum could provide advantage
  • Assess quantum readiness of your data and algorithms
  • Begin experimenting with quantum cloud services
  • Develop quantum literacy among technical staff
  • Evaluate cybersecurity implications and plan for post-quantum cryptography
  • Partner with quantum experts like Optic Bee Soft Tech

We work with all major quantum computing hardware approaches:

  • Superconducting qubits (IBM, Google, Rigetti)
  • Trapped ions (IonQ, Honeywell)
  • Photonic quantum computing (Xanadu)
  • Quantum annealing (D-Wave)
  • Topological qubits (Microsoft)
  • Neutral atoms (ColdQuanta, Pasqal)

We help clients select the best approach for their specific problem domain and work with hybrid quantum-classical architectures when appropriate.
